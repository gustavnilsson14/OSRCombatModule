Combat module alpha

Combat happens when people disagree violently, and is resolved in the following way, usually involving bruises, blood, and death.

The following are core concepts of the combat system.

  • Combat takes place on a 1d battle mat called the Battleground. You either push forward, or fall back.
  • Combat has two measures of time. Rounds, which consists of an arbitrary amount of Turns
  • Combatants can only specify their actions at the beginning of each Round, while during this time, their actions each Turn is dictated by their planning
  • Combat ends when at the end of a Round either one side is defeated, or has fled the battleground
  • Exploiting an enemys weaknesses is important to fighting effectively
  • Likewise, using terrain, features, and hazards of the battlegrounds is equally important
  • Combatants are likely to be permanently damaged, die, or be kidnapped in the aftermath of combat, players, and their opponents alike

The Battleground

The Battleground consists of 11 tiles, where two sides can duke it out. Each tile will have a width and a type of terrain, as well as possibly a feature, and a hazard

When the battle starts the GM first decides on the position of the two opposing parties, depending on the scene.

Movement

Combatants may move 1 tile at the start of every round if taking the dash tactical action, then 1 tile at the start of each turn.

These rules apply to movement

  • Conscious combatants of opposing sides may not occupy the same tile.
  • Combatants of the same side may occupy the same tile, and move freely through tiles occupied by their allies.
  • A combatant cannot move past an opponent.
  • Opposing combatants moving into the same tile roll 1d12, adding their strength to the result. The lower result will be unable to move

Depending on the combatants perks and/or equipment, they may be able to move further, or ignore some rules regarding invalid movement. These rules are also designed for mainly grounded combat. Aerial combat, or underwater combat would have different rules for movement.

Terrain

The terrain of a tile may increase or decrease damage of incoming ranged attacks, as well as increase the amount of movement required to cross it.

When a tile has a terrain which requires 2 or more movements to cross, resolve it as it was two tiles, and keep track of the combatants progress.

Tile width

A tile can only contain an amount of characters less than or equal to its width.

Features

A tile feature can be anything from a watchtower, to abandoned house, or maybe a ballista. Features are used by combatants to fortify, or push hard from certain positions on the battleground, to setting devious traps.

Interaction

Each feature has its own usage, but they may only be interacted with before the start of a new Round.

All features have a requirement of how to interact with them, from an amount of combatants to be present, to a certain damage type to hit them.

Scouting

Some features are face down as combat starts, and will only be discovered when scouted. A hidden feature is scouted when a combatant stands on the same tile as it.

A combatant can spend turns to increase their scouting radius while stationary, moving will reset their extended scouting radius. For each turn scouting, the combatant will scout features one tile further away from them.

Hazards

A tile can be hazardous to combatants. It might be on fire, have puddles of corrosive acid, toxic gas, or deep chasms dangerously close to the battle.

Each hazard is unique, might have a duration of rounds it is present, and will make any combatants starting a turn on the hazard suffer its negative effects.

Some equipment, and incantations may create hazards on the battleground

Guide

The turn is resolved in the following manner

  • Each melee combatant chooses a target in range, prioritizing their planned target. If the planned target is out of range, they attack a random target.
  • All combatants move.
  • All melee combatants attack. Melee targets which have moved out of range causes the attack to miss.
  • All unprepared ranged attackers prepare an attack
  • Prepared ranged attackers attack
  • Force is calculated where there are opposing fronts.

Step by step, this is how combat works.

  1. Battle is initiated, is any party ambushing the other?
  2. Plan your actions during the round. What is your target tile, and what will you do?
  3. Use equipment, and move
  4. Determine the amount of turns in the round depending on the players party first, then the opponents desired turn amount.
  5. If both parties decided for 0 turns, the combat ends, otherwise proceed to step 7.
  6. The first turn starts.
  7. Hazards affect the combatants on the same tile.
  8. All characters move.
  9. All characters attack.
  10. If the round has more turns, go to step 7, otherwise proceed to step 11.
  11. If one side is defeated, the combat ends, otherwise go to step 2.

Setup

As combat starts, all combatants of each respective side are placed on opposing sides, at tile #3. The GM may then, at their discretion move combatants forward, or backwards up to 2 tiles, depending on their perception of how the battle started.

First Strike

If a combatant was caught by surprise, with their weapons sheethed and guard down, the initiators may get a first strike. This allows any combatant who was combat ready as the combat starts to attack a non-combat ready combatant within range once.

Ambush

If either side ambushed the other side, seemingly springing up from nowhere, trapping or surrounding their opponents, the combat starts as an ambush.

When placing combatants on the battleground when an ambush happens, place the ambushed side on tile #6, and the ambushers on a tile #1 of their choice.

During the first round of an ambush, the ambushers choose how many turns it would last.

Rounds and Turns

A round of combat is an amount of turns decided by both the fighting parties. A turn of combat is enough time for every combatant involved to move, and perform an action.

Commitment

As a round of combat starts, the players must decide, and commit to how many turns they wish to fight, a number between 1-5. This is then offset by the enemy group's desired turn amount, and the average turn amount between the two is used, rounded in favor of the players.

Tactical action

After determining the turn amount for the round, each combatant may take a tactical action

Tactical actions resolve immediately, and simultaneously.

Push

A push action can only be used when in a front. When used, push adds 1 force to the combatants side in the front for this round.

Breakthrough

The breakthrough action allows a combatant to pass through an enemy front if they are less in number than the combatants front.

Equipment

The equipment action allows a combatant to freely move items between their belt, hands and the ground, as well as either use a single consumable item from the belt or hands, or move 1 item from the backpack into the hands or belt.

Escape

A combatant may escape the combat, if they fulfill the conditions explained in detail below.

Use Feature

Combatants may interact with the battleground features with this action. Each feature has its own unique effect when used.

Dash

The combatant may move 1 tile.

Cast spell

The spell action allows a combatant to cast one of their spells, explained in detail below.

Charge

The charge action increases the charge of 2 the combatants spells by 1. If the combatant only has 1 spell, the second point is forfeit.

Rage

The rage action causes attacks to ignore half of the combatants targets armor value, rounded in favor of the attacker.

Shrug

The shrug action allows the combatant to remove a condition they suffer from.

Scout

The scout action increases the combatants vision radius by 1.

Planning

At the start of each round, players plan what to do with the turns during the round.

This mainly consists of choosing where to attempt to move, which action to take and the target of said action.

Movement

Move to tile

A character may attempt to move to a tile. They will try to move towards that tile each turn with their movement.

Stand ground

A character may stand their ground, and forfeit their movement.

Charge

A charging character will keep attempting to move forward each turn.

Retreat

A retreating character will keep attempting to move backwards each turn.

Actions

Melee attack

Melee attacks can only be made onto adjacent tiles. They are performed each turn.

Ranged attack

Ranged attacks can be made onto any tile of the battleground unless otherwise specified by the weapon. Ranged attacks must be prepared, so they are completed every other turn.

Defend

The defend action will increase the armor of the combatant by 1, and allow the defender to temporarily remove one of their weaknesses for the round.
At the end of the round, one of their held weapons lose 1 durability.

Assist

A combatant may choose to assist an ally. When doing so, the assisted ally may choose to reroll all their damage dice for every attack they make during the round. The assisted character may not only reroll one of the dice this way, but must reroll all.

Focus

All players has 1 point of Focus as combat starts. A point of focus can be used during a round, at the start of a turn, to set a new plan for the rest of their round.

Escape

During combat, the losing side will more often than not want to escape the fight. To accomplish this, the combatants of the losing side must all succeed in escaping

For a combatant to escape, they must take the escape special action, and fulfill one of the following conditions;

  • All combatants of both sides want to escape, OR
  • The distance to the closest enemy, plus the amount of rounds the combat has lasted is greater than 7, OR
  • The distance to the closest enemy is greater than 11

After escaping the combatant will get away from their enemies, and automatically take a 10 minute break. After the break, the combatant will have their endurance fully restored.

Spells

Spells deal damage based on the characters skill with the spell, their experience level, and their current amount of charge. Spells target a tile, and deal their damage to all combatants on that tile.

Casting a spell consumes all its charges. Each charge spent adds another dice rolled for damage. The skill of the spell ranges from 1-5, and will improve the type of dice rolled, starting at 1d4 at 1 skill, ending at 1d12 at 5 skill. All spells cast adds the level damage dice to their damage.

When combat starts, all combatants recieve a single charge for a spell off their choice. The standard way to gain extra charges is by using charge as their tactical action.

After cast once, a spell cannot be used for the rest of the combat.

Dealing damage

Attacks are basic offensive actions taken to hurt an enemy combatants. Attacks in combat hit by default.

All attacks deal two or more dice of damage. The amount of faces on the standard three dice are based on the combatants weapon, their familiarity with that specific weapon, and their experience level.

Endurance

Endurance is a measure of how combat ready a character is. The more damage comes their way, the more endurance is spent to dodge or deflect that damage, until they are finally hit and suffer wounds.

As endurance reaches 0, it can be reduced no further, and instead the character suffers wounds, with one exception.

If any damage would result in a negative endurance less than or equal to a combatants negative max endurance, they die instantly.

Wounds

Each time a combatant is damaged when at 0 endurance they suffer a wound. The amount of wounds sustained have these effects.

Armor value

Characters and creatures are either unarmored, wear armor, or have natural armor.

When attacked, each die rolled for the attack is reduced by the armor value of the victim.

Weaknesses

All weapons deals damage of one or more damage types. All combatants have one or more weakness to these damage types. Figuring out an opponents weakness can be key to winning a battle

When dealing damage to an opponent with a damage type they are weak against this is considered critical damage. Dealing critical damage causes the attack to add the max value of your highest attack dice to the attack result. Also, armor does not protect against critical damage.

Fronts

When opposing melee combatants meet on the battleground a front is created. How melee attackers are allowed to attack, and how the front moves, has specific rules.

Rules of attacking

Before any opposing combatant in a front can be the target of two attacks during the same turn, every enemy combatant must be the target of an attack. Only if your side of the front outnumber the other side, you can target the same target as one of your allies.

Force

For each turn during a round, each combatant in a front which deals more damage than they suffer will add 1 point of force to their front.

At the end of the round, the side with most force may move forward, pushing the other side backwards.

Conditions

Conditions apply negative effects to a combatant which last until the end of the combat, or until removed by the tactical action shrug or other effect.

Conditions are applied as a combatant is hit by a weapon who may apply a condition to them. To apply a condition, the attacker must meet the following;

  • Deal critical damage with the attack, OR
  • Suffer one durability loss to their weapon before attempting the roll
  • The attack must deal damage, AND
  • No damage dice may show a 1, AND
  • The combatant has not applied a condition earlier this combat round.

There are 4 types of conditions, described below;

Physical affliction

Affects the characters strength and speed.

Mental distress

Affects the characters decision making.

Elemental scourge

Deals damage over time and additional effects

Finesse effect

Applies an instant effect, and is not tracked.

A list of all conditions by type, and their effects is found later on

Inventory

All things a character carries with them which are of note are represented by a item card. Item cards represent any weapons, protection, or tools the character carries and have at their disposal. The character sheet, and vehicle sheets has dedicated slots to contain item cards, which also denote how the item is carried.

Items carried in the characters hands or belt can be used in combat, while items carried on the characters body are items worn or strapped to them, and items in the 3 inventory slots are items stored in a backpack.

Item types

Stats

All items have stats, but depending on the type of the item the stats may mean different things.

Quality

The round slots are called quality. The quality of an item counts as both a durability, and an effect icon. Filling in the quality slot as the result of damaging the item will also result in the quality being lost for effect purposes.

Durability

The durability of an item is a measure of how much damage the item can withstand. When all durability, and quality slots are filled in as a result of damage, an item is destroyed.

Effect

The effect of an item counts towards either the damage rating, or armor value of the item. Quality also adds to this

Item damage

When an item is either used for somthing it is not intended for, suffers rough physical treatment, is partially or completely consumed, or due to certain combat actions and events, it takes damage.

When an item takes damage, cross out the first durability or quality slot (starting from the top left) which is not yet filled in.

Some items, at the GM's discretion are not destroyed when all durability or quality slots are filled in, and may be recharged or refilled, although this is an exception

Repair

Repairing a weapon may restore sturdy quality slots. Repairing a quality slot will take some time, and resources, usually worth around ~75% of what the quality slot would cost if new.

Weapons

There is a variety of weapons out there, from knives and clubs, to guns and grenades. The following is true for a weapon;

  • Weapons add a damage die to each attack. Which die depends on their damage rating
  • All weapons have a card, and needs to be held by a character to be used
  • Weapons have several damage types. If a weapon hits an enemy and has a damage type that combatant is weak against, it deals critical damage
  • All weapons can have one condition they may apply. These are not stated by the weapon cards, but added either randomly or at the discretion of the GM.
  • There are two types of weapons, melee and ranged
  • Quality and effect increases the weapons damage rating by 1
  • A weapons skill is used to track the players skill swith the individual weapon.

Adding conditions

As the player characters aquire a new weapon, it will either be a prepared weapon which the GM has already decided on the condition it applies, or an unprepared weapon the GM did not account for.

For weapons not prepared in advance, the GM decides on a condition type, and the player aquiring the weapon rolls a die on that condition types table to determine which condition the weapon applies.

Note down which condition the weapon may apply on the weapon card.

Protection

Different types of armor comes in shields, helmets, bodywear, or complete outfits. The following statements are true about armor;

  • There are four types of protection, armor, headgear, and shield
  • Each quality and effect increases armor value by 1
  • Quality is also used as durability for protection
  • The damage types of a protection item determines weaknesses the it mitigates
  • Armor occupying two card slots will occupy a characters body body item slots

Armor value

The total armor value of all equipped protection is added together when reducing the effect of an attack. Each damage die is reduced by the total armor value

Weakness mitigation

All damage types listed with a protection will mitigate the wearers weakness to that damage type. Additionally, the wearer can never become weak to that damage type as long as they wear the armor.

Tool

Tools are a common name for anything not a weapon, or protection. It is anything between batteries, water bottles, food, and egg timers.

Tools only has durability. Durability is reduced when tools is consumed, or used for something it is not made for

Character creation

To create a character requires you to make 3 rolls, one for each attribute, note them down on the character sheet. Then you should find, and note down the background corresponding to your total attribute value, and also find the three perks corresponding to your three individual attribute values.

Finally you should calculate your maximum endurance, and note that down on your character sheet.

Berserk

The combatant will charge the next round and they add 1d4 to their damage with melee weapons

Elemental scourges

Burning

The combatant takes 1d6 burn damage at the start of each turn. If weak to burn, the combatant will change their movement plan to retreat, and cannot take action during their turn.

Frozen

The combatant takes 1d6 cold damage at the start of each turn. If weak to cold, the combatant will change their movement plan to stand ground, and has 0 armor while frozen.

Electrocuted

The combatant takes 1d6 volt damage at the start of each turn. If weak to volt, the combatant drops all held items on the ground.

Hexxed

The combatant takes 1d6 vile damage at the start of each turn. If weak to vile, the combatant takes an additional 6 damage.

Blessed

The combatant takes 1d6 holy damage at the start of each turn. If weak to holy, the combatant takes an additional 6 damage.

Finesse effects

Weapon break

The weapon in the target's main hand takes one point of damage to durability.

Armor break

The armor the targetis wearing takes one point of damage to durability.

Disarm

The combatant drops the item in their main hand on the ground. Place it on their tile.
